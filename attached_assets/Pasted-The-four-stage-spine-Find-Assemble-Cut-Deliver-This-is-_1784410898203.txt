Where it slots in
Your current spine is Find → Assemble → Cut → Deliver. The clean fix is to make the naming honest about what each stage does to the material:

Find — locate footage (data in)
Assemble — arrange the story as beats/timecodes (data)
Refine — watch, trim, approve each beat (the new one)
Cut — render the approved story to video (media)
Deliver — export finished renders (media out)

The key reframe: Cut should only ever render what Refine approved. Right now Cut both generates and is the first time anyone sees actual footage. That's backwards. Refine is where a human lays eyes on the material before a single frame renders. It turns "generate and hope" into "review and commit."
If you want to keep four tabs, fold Refine into Assemble as a second mode (Arrange / Refine toggle)—but I'd argue it earns its own tab, because psychologically it's a different job: Assemble is ordering, Refine is watching.
What the Refine screen contains
Think of it as a stripped-down review-and-trim view built around your existing beat list, not a full NLE. You are not rebuilding Premiere. You're giving the editor just enough control to trust the cut.
Left: the beat list (you already have this). The 9 ordered beats from the Story. Clicking one loads it into the player. Current beat highlighted. Drag-to-reorder stays live here. Each beat shows its label and its current in/out timecode.
Center: the player. Plays the selected beat only, looped, with its current in/out as the trim boundaries. Below the scrubber, two draggable handles for in and out. This is the whole point—the editor watches the beat and nudges where it starts and ends. Frame-step buttons (or ← → keys) for precision. Show current in/out as editable timecode fields too, because some editors type faster than they drag.
The trim behavior that matters most: when they move the in-point, don't just trim—show them a second or two of handle on either side (the footage that exists before/after the current selection) so they can extend, not just shrink. Editors constantly need "give me two more seconds before she starts talking." If the only option is to cut tighter, the tool feels like a cage.
Right or below: the "why" panel. This is your trust fix. For the selected beat, show:

The transcript snippet covering that timecode (highlighted for the exact in/out range)
The match reason — why the system picked this range for this beat ("matched: finding redemption → strong semantic + emotional peak score")
The source file name

Now when the editor sees beat 3 is "God's Armor vs. Human Effort, 4:00–4:45," they read the transcript, see why, and either nod or drag the handles. That single panel converts a black box into a collaborator.
Per-beat approval. Each beat gets an Approve toggle (a checkmark). The Refine header shows "6 / 9 approved." Cut's render button stays disabled—or warns—until all beats are approved. That's your soft picture-lock: the checkpoint that stops endless backtracking. Once all nine are green, "Lock story" freezes it and unlocks Cut with confidence.
Interactions to include (and to deliberately leave out)
Include: reorder beats, trim in/out with handles, extend into surrounding footage, frame-step, relabel a beat, approve/reject per beat, and a "regenerate this beat" button (if a beat is wrong, let AI re-pick just that one against the same story intent—don't force a full rebuild).
Leave out (v1): transitions, effects, color, multi-track audio, J/L-cuts. The discipline from the research holds—don't polish in the rough/refine stage. Refine is about is this the right footage, in the right order, with the right boundaries. Nothing more. If you cram finishing tools in here you'll rebuild an NLE badly and dilute the one thing your tool does that Premiere doesn't.
The versioning piece (small addition, big payoff)
When they lock a story, snapshot it as a version. Producer says "swap beat 4"? They duplicate to v2, change the one beat, re-lock. Your "2 stories" stat already hints you support parallel versions—make lock-and-snapshot the mechanism. This also gives you the producer-feedback loop you're currently missing: each beat can hold a comment ("tighten the open") that shows up in Refine as a to-do.
The flow, end to end
Find footage → Assemble orders it into a story → Refine: watch each beat, adjust boundaries, read the why, approve → lock → Cut renders the locked story → Deliver exports. Human judgment sits exactly where editors demand it—at the boundary between "the machine assembled something" and "my name is on this"—and nowhere it isn't needed.
Build Refine and you've closed the one gap that separates an impressive auto-assembler from a tool an editor will actually stake their reputation on.
