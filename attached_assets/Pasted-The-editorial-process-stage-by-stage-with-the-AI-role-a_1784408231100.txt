The editorial process, stage by stage, with the AI role at each
Editing breaks into three macro-phases: Project Setup → Offline (creative) → Online (finishing). Here's each real stage, what the editor is actually doing, and where AI belongs.
1. Ingest & Organization
The editor pulls all footage into the NLE, sets up folder structure, and establishes naming conventions. This is unglamorous and hated but load-bearing: taking the time to lay out folder structure and file naming conventions in a logical, consistent fashion pays dividends later — every time you need to find something it's quick. LucidLink
AI role — high autonomy, this is pure grind: Auto-transcribe everything, auto-tag by content, detect scenes, identify and label speakers, sync multicam, flag technical problems (focus, audio clipping, shaky shots). Critically, solve the metadata-consistency problem: metadata quality otherwise hinges entirely on the individual logging the footage — a new intern vs. a veteran — yielding different vocabularies and tagging styles that render cross-team search useless. A consistent AI logger fixes this structurally. Iconik
2. Logging & Review (the biggest time sink)
Traditionally the editor watches everything and notes selects. In high-volume work — reality, docs, news, sports, multi-cam corporate — this is impossible to do fully. Television runs on footage that almost no one has time to watch; productions shoot far more material than editors can reasonably process. mexc
AI role — high value, keep human in the loop: This is your killer feature. Turn the footage into structured, searchable material with story beats identified, labeled and organized. Two capabilities matter most: mexc

Semantic search over meaning, not filenames. Traditional metadata-based search often fails to find specific objects, scenes, or emotions, resulting in frustration and wasted time. Let an editor type "the moment she gets emotional talking about her father" and get the clip. Zilliz
Select/beat scoring. A heat map that scores every beat so the editor knows where the gold is before watching. But surface why a clip scored high and let them override — never hide the reasoning.

3. Rough Cut / Assembly
The editor builds the skeleton. The aim of the rough cut is to get a good idea of the order before applying big cuts or effects — don't worry about getting things perfect. The goal is getting the story structure working without worrying about the rough edges. TechSmithLucidLink
AI role — draft generator, not decision-maker: This is where the industry has landed in 2026. Adobe's "Quick Cut" automates the initial assembly, analyzing footage for the best takes and assembling a rough draft based on the editor's specified duration and narrative prompt. Text-based editing (edit the transcript, the timeline follows) is now standard. Your system should generate a starting assembly the editor tears apart — a first draft to react to, which is psychologically far easier than a blank timeline. Guardrail: a critical discipline is not polishing during rough cut. The two most common causes of rework are skipping formal picture lock and polishing shots during the rough cut phase. Your AI should resist the temptation to over-finish the assembly. Digen AIBonomotion
4. Fine Cut
Pure craft. The editor works pacing, rhythm, J-cuts and L-cuts, B-roll placement, emotional beats. Creative instinct matters enormously in the fine cut and finishing stages. Bonomotion
AI role — minimal, assistive only: Back off. Offer suggestions on request (alternate takes, B-roll matches, gap-filling), surface options, but the editor drives. This is the "human-final" half of the workflow and the part they're proud of. Intruding here is how you lose trust.
5. Picture Lock
A formal checkpoint: the edit is frozen so finishing can begin. Enforcing it prevents chaos. Declaring picture lock formally and resisting any polish until the fine cut is approved eliminates the majority of backtracking. Bonomotion
AI role: Workflow enforcement and change-tracking, not creative.
6. Finishing — Color, Sound, VFX, Graphics (Online)
Color correction adjusts balance, exposure and contrast for consistency; color grading is the creative process of enhancing colors to create a specific mood. Plus sound design, titles, and cleanup. Order matters here: color grading always comes after the assembly is locked, because it would be pointless to grade footage that won't be used. Simon Says AIArtlist
AI role — high autonomy on the mechanical, human on the aesthetic: Automate the tedious cleanup — masking and generative fill to remove distracting objects across a clip, reducing tedious rotoscoping, base color matching across shots, auto-captions, resizing/reframing for platforms. Leave the look (the creative grade, the mood) to the human or make it a one-click starting point they adjust. Digen AI
7. Review & Delivery
Feedback rounds with producers/clients, then export to spec. Feedback merges into the timeline so editors resolve comments frame-by-frame without losing context. Ziflow
AI role: Ingest and localize timestamped feedback, auto-generate delivery variants per platform/spec, archive with searchable metadata so the asset stays findable later.
